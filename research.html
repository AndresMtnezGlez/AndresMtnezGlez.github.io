Research & Notes

Technical notes, replications and R code on macroeconometrics, Bayesian methods and statistical techniques, by Andres Martinez Gonzalez, economist at AIReF.

A working collection of technical notes on the econometric methods I use. Most began as graduate coursework or as replications of papers I wanted to understand properly, and each one is a self-contained document: the theory, the code, and the output in one place, so the results can be reproduced by running the file.

All source is written in Quarto and available on GitHub.

Vector autoregressions and structural identification

How VAR models are estimated, identified and read. These notes cover the Wold representation and impulse response functions, structural identification through Cholesky and A/B models, and replications of two papers that shaped how monetary policy shocks are measured — Christiano, Eichenbaum and Evans (1998) and Bernanke, Boivin and Eliasz (2005) — alongside an application to the response of the labour share to productivity shocks.

Replicating Bernanke, Boivin and Eliasz (2005): A FAVAR Approach in R

A replication in R of Bernanke, Boivin and Eliasz (2005), building a factor-augmented VAR (FAVAR) by extracting principal components from a large macroeconomic dataset and tracing the effects of monetary policy shocks.

Mar 13, 2021

Replicating Christiano, Eichenbaum and Evans (1998): Monetary Policy Shocks in a VAR

A step-by-step replication in R of Christiano, Eichenbaum and Evans (1998), estimating a three-variable VAR in output growth, inflation and the Federal Funds Rate, with reduced-form SUR estimation, recursive identification and impulse response functions to a monetary policy shock.

Mar 6, 2021

Does Productivity Move the Labour Share? Overshooting and Structural Identification in a Bivariate VAR

A hands-on macroeconometric study of how the labour share responds to productivity shocks, replicating the overshooting result of Rios-Rull and Santaeulalia-Llopis (2010). Covers VAR estimation and lag selection, structural identification through A and B models, impulse response functions, forecast error variance decomposition, and a robustness check using an alternative definition of the labour share.

Nov 14, 2020

A Notebook on VAR Models: Wold Representation, Impulse Response Functions and Identification

Lecture-style notes deriving the vector moving average (Wold) representation of a VAR, structural identification via Cholesky decomposition, and impulse response functions, with worked examples in R using the vars package.

Nov 13, 2020

Bayesian and simulation methods

Estimating posterior distributions when there is no analytical solution. These notes build the Gibbs sampler and the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm from scratch in R, and cover discrete-time Markov chains and the Tauchen method for discretising an AR(1) process.

The Gibbs Sampler: Bayesian Estimation of a Normal Model in R

An introduction to the Gibbs sampler, an MCMC method for Bayesian estimation when analytical solutions are not available. Derives the conditional posteriors for a normal model with unknown mean and precision, implements the algorithm in R, and applies it to the Howell height data.

May 1, 2023

Metropolis-Hastings from Scratch: Bayesian Estimation of an AR(1) Model in R

A practical guide to the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm, building intuition through the Good King Markov parable before implementing it in R to estimate an AR(1) model. Compares direct sampling from a Normal-inverted-Gamma posterior with the random-walk Metropolis-Hastings sampler, covering proposal distributions, acceptance probabilities and convergence.

Apr 15, 2021

A Notebook on Markov Chains: Transition Matrices, Stationary Distributions and Tauchen Discretisation

Notes on discrete-time Markov chains in R, covering transition probabilities, n-step transition matrices, stationary distributions, estimating a transition matrix from data, and discretising an AR(1) process with the Tauchen method.

Nov 20, 2020

An Introduction to Principal Component Analysis: Covariance, Eigenvectors and Eigenvalues

An introduction to principal component analysis (PCA), deriving the first principal component as a constrained variance-maximisation problem and explaining the three building blocks it rests on: the covariance matrix, eigenvectors, and eigenvalues, with worked numerical examples.
